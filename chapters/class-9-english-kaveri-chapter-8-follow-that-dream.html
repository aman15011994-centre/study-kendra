Chapter Summary

"Follow That Dream" is an inspiring letter written by Irene Chua to her teenage daughter, Ming, taken from the collection 'My Daughter, My Friend'. The text beautifully highlights the deep relationship between a mother and her child while sharing profound guidance about achieving personal aspirations in life.

The letter opens with the heartening words, "By all means follow that dream." The mother encourages Ming to chase her aspirations, explaining that achievers reach greatness through rigorous effort and sacrifice. She emphasizes that reaching a world-class standard in any field requires a singular, intensive commitment for at least ten years. The process of pursuing a dream begins with passion, transforms into strong conviction, and demands an honest calculation of emotional and financial costs before one fully "plunges" into it.

Furthermore, the mother reminds Ming that success is never a solitary journey. No one succeeds completely alone; a strong support network is essential, similar to Academy Award winners who thank their families and mentors. She also highlights that many people fail to realize their dreams because they remain stuck in wishful thinking, or because unexpected circumstances—like the Japanese invasion during World War World II—alter their destiny. Ultimately, the letter teaches the crucial lesson of balancing passion with practical planning, proving that dreams can change over time as one navigates life's uphill challenges.

Important Questions and Answers

Short Answer Questions (1–10)

1. Who wrote the letter in the chapter and to whom?

The letter was written by Irene Chua to her daughter, Ming. It is taken from the book 'My Daughter, My Friend'.

2. What is the date of the letter?

The letter is dated 19 June 1995.

3. How does the mother begin her letter?

She begins by telling Ming, "By all means follow that dream," encouraging her to pursue her ambition.

4. According to the mother, how long does it take to reach world-class standard in a field?

She says one must singularly and intensively pursue a subject for at least ten years to reach world-class standard.

5. What three things make up the "cost" of pursuing a dream?

The cost includes years of effort, financial investment, and sacrifice.

6. What does the mother say about people who never fulfil their dreams?

She says many people only wish for things but never move beyond wishful thinking, and never act on their dreams.

7. Give one reason why some people's destinies changed, as mentioned in the letter.

The Japanese invasion during World War II changed the destiny of people who had dreamed of studying at Raffles College.

8. What is the mother's own example of a chased dream?

Publishing her book was a dream the mother chased for the last ten years, though it wasn't her original childhood dream.

9. What does the phrase "wet blanket" mean as used in the chapter?

It means a spoil-sport, someone who dampens enthusiasm or discourages others.

10. What is the tone of the mother throughout the letter?

The tone is caring, honest, and encouraging, balancing hope with realistic caution.

Medium Answer Questions (11–20)

11. What role does passion and conviction play in achieving a dream, according to the letter?

The mother explains that pursuing a dream starts with passion for a particular interest. This passion then turns into conviction — a strong belief that it is necessary to realise it. Once the person has weighed the cost in effort, money, and sacrifice, and the desire still burns within them, they should commit fully and "plunge" into the task.

12. How does the mother describe the journey towards achieving a dream?

She describes the journey as mostly "uphill," meaning full of struggle and difficulty. She says people are often kept going only by the knowledge that they are doing what they love and doing the right thing, and that the prospect of success helps them continue even when their stamina runs low.

13. Why does the mother mention the Academy Awards in her letter?

She mentions the Academy Awards to show that behind every successful person, there is a group of people who supported them. Winners always thank a host of people who formed their support network, proving that no one achieves success completely alone.

14. What examples does the mother give of dreams that were never fulfilled?

She mentions people whose dream was to study at Raffles College but whose lives were changed by the Japanese invasion during World War II. She also mentions people who wished to finish secondary school but had to work instead, to support their siblings' education.

15. How does the mother balance encouragement with caution in her letter?

While she strongly encourages Ming to follow her dream, she also cautions her to realistically consider the years of effort, sacrifice, and financial cost involved. She says she is "not going to put a wet blanket" on the dream, but wants Ming to think it through carefully before committing.

16. How has the mother's own dream changed over the years?

The mother shares that her original dream in her youth was different from what she pursued later in life. Publishing her book became her new dream, which she chased for ten years, showing that dreams can evolve with time and circumstances.

17. What does the mother mean by saying "life itself may change a person's dreams"?

She means dreams are not fixed forever; circumstances, experiences, and time can shape new hopes and aspirations. These new dreams are just as valuable as the dreams of one's younger days, even if they take longer to achieve.

18. Why does achieving a dream require more people than one might expect?

As dreams take longer to fulfil and pass through many hurdles, more people become involved in supporting the journey — hence the mother says "the people who are participants in your dreamscape would be many more."

19. What is the significance of the title "Follow That Dream"?

The title reflects the central message of the letter — the mother's encouragement to her daughter to pursue her dream wholeheartedly, while also being aware of its real costs and challenges.

20. Why do you think the mother chose to write this advice in the form of a letter?

A letter allows the mother to express personal, heartfelt advice directly to her daughter in an intimate way. It creates a warm, one-on-one connection, making the guidance feel more personal and sincere than formal advice.

Long Answer Questions (21–25)

21. Explain, in your own words, how the mother believes a dream can be transformed into reality.

The mother explains that transforming a dream into reality is a step-by-step process. It begins with a genuine passion for something, followed by a strong conviction that pursuing it is necessary. Before committing, one must count the true cost — years of effort, money, and personal sacrifice. Only if the desire is still strong after this honest assessment should a person commit fully, or "plunge," into the pursuit. The journey afterward is difficult and uphill, and one is kept going by the belief that they are doing what they love and doing the right thing. When stamina runs low, the hope of eventual success keeps a person on track. She also emphasises that no one succeeds alone — a strong support network is essential, as seen in her Academy Awards example.

22. Describe the differences the mother highlights between mere dreamers and actual achievers.

The mother draws a clear distinction between those who merely dream and those who achieve. Dreamers often say wistfully, "I wish I could be this or the other," but never move beyond wishful thinking. They may prefer security over risk, or their circumstances may change, preventing them from pursuing their goals. Achievers, on the other hand, take the passion they feel and convert it into a firm conviction, accepting the real costs of effort, time, and sacrifice. They commit fully to their goal despite an uphill journey, staying motivated by their love for the work and belief in doing the right thing. This willingness to act, sacrifice, and persist — rather than just imagine — is what separates achievers from dreamers.

23. How does the mother use personal anecdotes and real-world examples to strengthen her message?

The mother uses several examples to make her advice believable and relatable. She refers to Academy Award winners who thank their support networks, showing that success needs a community of people. She recalls people whose dream was to study at Raffles College, but whose plans were disrupted by the Japanese invasion during World War II, illustrating how uncontrollable circumstances can alter destinies. She also mentions people who had to leave school early to support their siblings, showing that responsibilities can change a person's path. Finally, she shares her personal experience of publishing her book after chasing that dream for ten years, proving through her own life that dreams can shift and still be achieved with dedication.

24. Do you think the mother's advice about pursuing dreams is still relevant today? Give reasons.

Yes, this advice remains highly relevant in contemporary times. Young people today still face the challenge of choosing between passion and security, much like the examples the mother gives. The idea that achieving excellence requires years of consistent effort applies to modern fields like technology, sports, and the arts. Her point about needing a support network is also true today, as mentors, family, and friends continue to play an important role in helping people achieve their goals. However, circumstances that disrupt dreams — such as financial difficulties or unexpected life events — are still common today, making her balanced advice of hope combined with realistic planning valuable for readers of any generation.

25. What lesson does the letter teach about balancing passion with practicality?

The letter teaches that following a dream requires both emotional commitment and practical planning. The mother does not simply tell Ming to blindly chase her dream; she asks her to genuinely evaluate the years of effort, sacrifice, and financial investment involved before committing. This balance shows maturity — passion alone is not enough; one must be prepared for hardship and be realistic about what the journey demands. At the same time, she reassures Ming that if the desire is strong enough after this honest assessment, she should not hold back. This balanced approach of encouraging ambition while promoting thoughtful preparation is the central lesson of the letter.
