Poem Summary

"Words" by Charles Swain is a thoughtful poem exploring the limitations of spoken language. The poet contrasts the transient nature of empty eloquence with the lasting value of sincere, truthful speech. He illustrates that massive "hosts of words" fail to offer true comfort to the human heart.

Using vivid natural analogies, Swain compares fleeting words to summer birds that fly away, leaving only empty air. In contrast, "a little said, and truly said" brings immense joy, comforting the weary heart. The poem concludes with a powerful comparison: empty words are like showy, gaudy plants that look attractive but produce no useful fruit.

Important Questions and Answers

Short Answer Questions (1–10)

1. Who is the poet of the poem "Words"?

The poem "Words" is written by Charles Swain, a prominent nineteenth-century English poet and writer.

2. To what does the poet compare words in the first stanza?

The poet compares words to summer birds. Just like migratory birds fly away after summer, words depart quickly, leaving only empty air.

3. What remains after spoken words depart?

After spoken words depart, they leave nothing of substance behind, only "empty air," providing no lasting comfort.

4. Why is the heart called "a pilgrim upon earth"?

The heart is called a pilgrim because, like a weary traveller journeying through life, it is constantly searching for true comfort and emotional satisfaction.

5. What are words compared to when the heart needs real comfort?

In times of genuine emotional distress, words are described as being as worthless and unhelpful as weeds in a garden.

6. What can "a little said, and truly said" achieve?

A few words that are sincere and truthful can bring deeper joy to a person than a massive quantity of empty speech.

7. Where do "hosts of words" reach according to the poet?

An abundance of empty words can reach only the head (intellectual understanding) but they never touch the heart (emotional connection).

8. What impact does a small but sincere voice have on a lonely home?

A sincere voice wins its "sunny way" with ease into a lonely, comforting home, bringing light, warmth, and joy even when speaking very few words.

9. What would happen if words could satisfy the chest?

If words could truly satisfy the heart's deepest desires and worries, the entire world would celebrate with a grand feast.

10. What happens when words are "summoned to the test"?

When words are put to the trial of offering genuine emotional support, they fail and satisfy the least.

Medium Answer Questions (11–20)

11. Explain the comparison of words with summer birds.

Swain compares words to summer birds to emphasize their temporary and superficial nature. Just as migratory birds stay during pleasant weather and depart when the season changes, words are spoken easily but vanish quickly without leaving any real or lasting support behind.

12. Why does the poet compare words to weeds?

The heart of a person in distress is compared to a traveler or pilgrim. In times of sadness, empty words fail to provide real solace. Therefore, the poet declares that ordinary, superficial words are as worthless as unwanted, unhelpful weeds.

13. What is the difference between words that "reach the head" and those that "touch the heart"?

Words that "reach the head" are intellectually understood but lack sincerity and fail to create an emotional bond. Conversely, words that "touch the heart" are sincere, deeply felt, and possess the ability to soothe, heal, and bring true joy.

14. How does the poet show that a small number of words can be extremely precious?

The poet gives the example of a voice that enters a quiet, lonely home. This comforting voice speaks very few words, yet those words are incredibly valuable because they are spoken with genuine warmth, bringing sunny cheer to the environment.

15. Explain the line: "If words could satisfy the chest, / The world might hold a feast."

These lines express a hypothetical scenario. If mere words were capable of resolving human worries and satisfying the heart, there would be no sorrow, and the entire human race would celebrate happily as if attending a giant feast.

16. What does the poet mean by "summoned to the test"?

"Summoned to the test" refers to situations when a person is experiencing real adversity or deep grief. It is during these times of trial that empty words are exposed because they are unable to provide any actual comfort or reassurance.

17. Describe the metaphor of "plants that make a gaudy show" in the final stanza.

The poet compares empty talkers to plants that put on a bright, showy display of flowers (gaudy show) but have no depth. Despite their attractive appearance, they never bear even a tiny particle of actual fruit, representing superficial talk with zero substance.

18. How does the poem highlight the theme of sincerity in communication?

The poem argues that excessive talking or grand vocabulary is meaningless without honest feeling. True communication requires sincerity; a few simple, honest words can bring profound joy, whereas a flood of insincere words produces nothing of value.

19. Why does the poet consider the human heart a pilgrim?

The heart is called a pilgrim because it is on a spiritual and emotional journey through a world of challenges. Like a traveler seeking shelter, the heart searches for genuine love and comfort, making empty words highly disappointing.

20. What is the role of natural imagery (birds, weeds, plants) in the poem?

Swain uses natural elements to make his abstract ideas concrete. Birds represent fleeting words, weeds symbolize worthlessness, and showy, fruitless plants represent empty promises, creating a vivid, easily understandable picture for the readers.

Long Answer Questions (21–25)

21. Discuss the central theme of the poem "Words" by Charles Swain.

The central theme of the poem revolves around the limits of spoken language and the importance of sincerity over quantity. Swain argues that empty, excessive talking cannot heal human suffering. Words can be fleeting and temporary, like migratory birds. True comfort does not lie in impressive or flowery speeches that only satisfy the logical mind. Instead, the heart craves genuine connection. "A little said, and truly said" possesses the incredible power to bring lasting joy and comfort. By contrasting insincere showiness with simple, heartfelt truth, the poem teaches us that honest, meaningful, and thoughtful communication is the only thing that holds true emotional worth.

22. Analyze the natural imagery and metaphors used by Charles Swain to illustrate the limitations of words.

Charles Swain masterfully utilizes rich natural imagery and metaphors to highlight how spoken words often fail to meet our emotional needs. First, he employs the simile of "summer birds" that depart quickly, leaving behind "empty air" to illustrate how fleeting spoken words are. Second, the human heart is personified as a tired "pilgrim upon earth," seeking spiritual comfort but finding words to be as useless as "weeds." Finally, the poet compares insincere speech to a "gaudy show" of showy plants that blossom beautifully but are of a "poor nature" that cannot produce "one particle of fruit." These natural symbols collectively demonstrate that without genuine feeling, words remain hollow, superficial, and unproductive.

23. How does the poem distinguish between the quantity and quality of spoken words? Support with examples.

The poem draws a sharp line of distinction between the volume of speech and its true quality. Swain points out that "hosts of words" are often useless because they only "reach the head" but fail to "touch the heart." Having a lot of words may sound impressive, but it offers no real solace during a crisis. In contrast, the poet praises quality through the phrase "a little said, and truly said," which imparts a "deeper joy." This is supported by the example of a soft voice that enters a "lonely home." Though this voice has "the fewest words to say," those few words are extremely "dear" and bring sunny warmth, proving that sincere, honest words outvalue endless empty chatter.

24. Explain the significance of the final stanza comparing empty words to showy but fruitless plants.

The final stanza serves as a powerful concluding metaphor for the entire poem. Swain compares empty words to "plants that make a gaudy show." These plants might capture attention with their bright, colorful blossoms, but their root is weak and their nature is poor, making them incapable of growing "one particle of fruit." In human terms, people who speak beautifully but insincerely are like these showy plants. Their speech lacks the "fruit" of actual empathy, comfort, or action. This imagery warns readers against being swayed by superficial eloquence and highlights that true value is found in productive, sincere, and practical kindness rather than empty, decorative displays of language.

25. What moral values or life lessons can readers learn from the poem "Words"?

The poem provides essential lessons about mindfulness, honesty, and empathy in our interactions. It teaches us that we should prioritize the truthfulness and sincerity of our speech over mere talkativeness. In our daily lives, we often use too many empty words that do not carry any real emotion. Swain reminds us that in times of grief, people do not need elaborate, long-winded speeches; they need simple, heartfelt words of true care. By practicing speaking "a little... and truly," we learn to communicate with genuine empathy and integrity. This fosters deeper, more authentic human relationships and prevents us from becoming superficially "showy" like the fruitless plants described in the poem.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who is the poet of the poem Words in Class 9 English Kaveri?

The poem Words is written by Charles Swain. It appears in Unit 7 of the Class 9 English textbook Kaveri, on pages 220 to 230.

What is the central theme of the poem Words by Charles Swain?

The central theme of the poem is that the true value of words lies not in their quantity but in their sincerity and truthfulness. A few heartfelt genuine words are more valuable than a large number of empty or meaningless words.

To what does the poet compare words in the first stanza of the poem Words?

The poet compares words to summer birds that depart quickly after the season ends, leaving behind only empty air. This shows that spoken words are temporary and do not leave lasting comfort.

Why is the heart called a pilgrim upon earth in the poem Words?

The heart is called a pilgrim upon earth because like a traveller journeying through life in search of fulfilment, the heart too keeps searching for true comfort and meaning, often finding that ordinary words fail to satisfy its deepest needs.

What does the image of plants that make a gaudy show represent in the poem Words?

The image represents empty or insincere words. Just as such plants look attractive but produce no real fruit, empty words may sound pleasing but fail to produce any genuine emotional comfort or meaningful outcome.

What poetic devices are used in the poem Words by Charles Swain?

The main poetic devices used include simile (words compared to summer birds), metaphor (heart described as a pilgrim), repetition (the word words repeated throughout), and hyperbole (if words could satisfy the chest, the world might hold a feast).

What does a little said and truly said mean in the poem Words?

It means that a small number of sincere and truthful words can bring far deeper joy and comfort than a large quantity of meaningless or empty words. Quality and sincerity matter more than the quantity of words spoken.

What is the rhyme scheme of the poem Words by Charles Swain?

The poem Words by Charles Swain follows an ABAB rhyme scheme in each stanza. For example in the first stanza: heart (A), care (B), depart (A), air (B). This alternate rhyme pattern continues throughout all six stanzas.
