Poem Summary

"Believe in Yourself" by Robert Langley is a motivational poem from Unit 8 of Class 9 English Kaveri. The poem challenges readers to pursue their dreams with courage and self-belief. The poet emphasizes that facing challenges is a solitary journey where it is just the individual and their future, with no crowd to watch or support.

While staying in the comfortable "status quo" is easy, it prevents personal growth. The poet reminds us that the first step towards change is always the hardest, requiring immense bravery. However, once that crucial step is taken, there is no turning back. Ultimately, deep self-belief is what keeps the future on track, turning obstacles into opportunities.

Important Questions and Answers

Short Answer Questions (1–10)

1. Who is the poet of "Believe in Yourself," and on what pages does it appear?

The poem is written by Robert Langley and appears on pages 245–246 of Unit 8 of the Class 9 English textbook Kaveri.

2. According to the poem, who watches you as you step up to a challenge?

According to the poem, there is no crowd to watch; it is just the individual and their future.

3. What two things may happen with the future, according to stanza 2?

The future may either pull you forward or push you back in fear, acting as a force of progression or stagnation.

4. Why do choices become difficult as the future gets near, according to the poem?

Choices become difficult as the future gets near because of the growing fear and uncertainty that manifests as decision time approaches.

5. What does the poet say about comfort in stanza 3?

The poet states that there is ease in maintaining the status quo, but comfort is not how we grow.

6. According to the poem, what is the hardest part of any journey?

According to the poem, the first step towards change or a new destination is the hardest.

7. What happens once the first step is taken, according to the poem?

Once the first step is taken, there is no turning back; you are fully committed to your path.

8. What is needed to keep the future "on track," according to the poem?

Believing in yourself and maintaining firm self-confidence is needed to keep the future on track.

9. Who wrote the companion poem "Always Believe in Yourself," and where does it appear?

The companion poem "Always Believe in Yourself" was written by Dorothy Hewitt and appears on page 258.

10. According to "Always Believe in Yourself," what should we hold on to when tested beyond our endurance?

We should hold on to courage, continue, and persist with open hands and an open heart when tested beyond our endurance.

Medium Answer Questions (11–20)

11. What does the line "There is no crowd to see" suggest about facing challenges?

The line suggests that facing a challenge is a solitary journey. There is no audience or outside support; only the individual's own effort and internal courage matter when stepping up.

12. What does the line "It's just you and the future" suggest?

This line suggests that facing the future is a deeply personal responsibility that cannot be shared. The individual alone must decide and act on their own path without relying on others.

13. Explain why "The first step is the hardest" is a metaphor.

This line is metaphorical because it does not refer to a literal physical stride; instead, it represents the psychological courage and effort needed to initiate any change or new beginning in life.

14. Identify the lines from the poem that show antithesis and explain why.

The lines contrasting "ease in comfort" to "maintain the status quo" with "this isn't how we grow" show antithesis. They pair the opposite ideas of comfortable stagnation and necessary growth to highlight the difference.

15. How is the speaker of the poem best described?

The speaker is best described as a guide who understands the internal struggle of decision-making. He directly addresses the reader as "You" to create a close, conversational connection that inspires action.

16. What does the phrase "push you back in fear" evoke?

This phrase evokes the invisible mental barriers that prevent growth. It illustrates how fear acts as a regressive force, pushing a person back into safety and keeping them from achieving their dreams.

17. As per the poem's symbolism, what do comfort and the status quo represent?

Comfort and the status quo represent stagnation, inertia, and the fear of change. They symbolize the "comfort zone" that holds a person back from fulfilling their true potential.

18. Contrast Robert Langley's poem with Dorothy Hewitt's "Always Believe in Yourself."

Robert Langley's poem focuses on the solitary nature of facing challenges and taking the first step. Dorothy Hewitt's poem expands on this by advising readers to be kind to themselves, persist through endurance tests, and keep their minds open.

19. What is a rhetorical question, and how is it used in the poem?

A rhetorical question is asked for dramatic effect rather than to elicit an answer. The poet asks "Will it pull you forward / Or push you back in fear?" to force readers to reflect on how they handle their future.

20. What is the overall tone of the poem, and how does it shift?

The overall tone is highly motivational and encouraging. It shifts from thoughtful reflection on fear and comfort in the beginning to firm, unwavering determination and confidence by the final stanza.

Long Answer Questions (21–25)

21. Detail the central ideas of each of the four stanzas in "Believe in Yourself."

The first stanza establishes that confronting life's hurdles is a lonely endeavor, where only you can face your future. The second stanza explores the psychological duality of the future, which can either propel you forward or drive you back in fear as choices become harder. The third stanza critiques the "status quo," explaining that staying in your comfort zone is easy but prevents necessary personal growth. The fourth stanza concludes with an encouraging resolution, emphasizing that while the first step is the hardest, absolute self-belief is the only force capable of keeping your future on track.

22. Discuss the use of antithesis, metaphor, and symbolism in the poem.

The poet makes rich use of literary devices. Antithesis is used to contrast "ease in comfort" with the struggle required to "grow," highlighting that comfortable stability and progress are opposites. The "first step" serves as a metaphor for initiating life-changing decisions. Additionally, the "future" symbolizes the vast, untested potential of an individual, while the "crowd" symbolizes external distractions that one must block out to find true self-belief. These devices make the poem's message both vivid and deeply analytical.

23. Critically evaluate whether simple self-belief is sufficient to overcome life's obstacles.

While the poem suggests that "believing in yourself" is what keeps the future on track, realistic planning, hard work, and persistence are also essential. Self-belief provides the spark of courage needed to take that incredibly difficult first step. However, once on the path, a person also needs resilience and sometimes a support network (as emphasized in companion texts). Thus, self-belief is a vital catalyst, but it must be paired with consistent action to truly conquer life's diverse challenges.

24. Analyze the transition of tone from thoughtful hesitation to firm determination throughout the stanzas.

The poem begins with a quiet, reflective tone, addressing the solitary nature of challenges ("no crowd to see"). It introduces hesitation in the second stanza, acknowledging that choices get difficult and fear can push us back. However, the tone shifts dramatically in the third stanza, becoming critical of comfort and the status quo. By the fourth stanza, all hesitation is gone; the tone is completely assertive and determined, declaring that once the step is taken, there is no turning back, ending the poem on a highly empowering note.

25. Relate the lessons of "Believe in Yourself" to real-world achievers who turned struggles into success.

The lesson of moving past comfort and embracing hard paths is reflected in the lives of historical leaders. Dr. B. R. Ambedkar faced immense social challenges but chose education to reform society. Lal Bahadur Shastri rose from humble origins with quiet determination, and Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam believed in his potential to lead India's space and defense sectors. These individuals proved Langley's core message: by taking the hard first step and maintaining absolute self-belief, they successfully turned immense obstacles into historic achievements.
