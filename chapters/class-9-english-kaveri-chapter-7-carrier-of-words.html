Chapter Summary

The chapter "Carrier of Words" is a deeply moving and inspiring account of Khetaram, a dedicated Gramin Dak Sewak (GDS) who serves the remote, isolated villages in the harsh Thar Desert of Rajasthan near the Indo-Pakistan border. In a region where communication facilities are extremely limited, Khetaram acts as the sole lifeline connecting the villagers with their families working far away. Every day, he braves extreme weather conditions, including scorching temperatures exceeding 45 degrees Celsius, sandstorms, and shifting sand dunes, to deliver letters, money orders, and pensions.

Khetaram's role extends far beyond that of a traditional postman. Since many villagers are illiterate, he reads their incoming letters aloud and patiently writes their replies. He shares in their moments of joy and handles painful news, such as letters of death, with deep compassion and respect for local customs. Khetaram chose this difficult job to secure a stable livelihood for his family when farming became impossible due to frequent droughts. His selfless dedication, honesty, and emotional support have earned him the immense trust, love, and respect of the entire desert community, showing how ordinary people can perform extraordinary services.

Important Questions and Answers

Short Answer Questions (1–10)

1. Who is Khetaram and what is his role?

Khetaram is a Gramin Dak Sewak (GDS) working in the Thar Desert. He delivers letters, money orders, and other postal articles, and also reads incoming mail and writes replies for illiterate villagers.

2. Why is Khetaram called the 'sole link' between the villagers and their families?

Many villages in the Thar Desert have limited communication and transportation facilities. Khetaram regularly delivers mail and money orders, serving as the sole physical bridge between separated families.

3. What difficulties does Khetaram face while delivering mail?

He walks long distances across difficult, shifting sand dunes in extreme desert heat and swirling sandstorms while carrying a heavy mailbag.

4. Why does Khetaram sometimes postpone his deliveries?

During extremely hot summer afternoons, traveling across sand dunes is physically dangerous. He rests and waits until sundown to continue his journey safely.

5. Why is the role of Gramin Dak Sewaks considered important?

They provide essential postal, financial, and savings services to remote and structurally inaccessible villages that lack regular communication systems.

6. Why did Khetaram choose the job of a Gramin Dak Sewak?

In the drought-prone Thar Desert, farming alone is highly unstable and cannot provide a secure income. The postman job gave him a stable, reliable livelihood.

7. How do the villagers show their trust in Khetaram?

They confidently ask him to read their personal letters, write replies, handle their financial money orders, and always welcome him warmly into their homes.

8. Which type of letter does Khetaram fear the most? Why?

He fears delivering letters carrying news of a relative's death because such news brings severe sorrow and grief to the families and leaves a heavy emotional impact on him.

9. How do the BSF personnel help Khetaram?

The Border Security Force (BSF) personnel often offer him transport lifts in their vehicles along desert tracks and receive him warmly when he delivers their official mail.

10. Why does the author pay tribute to people like Khetaram?

The author honors them for their selfless service, persistent effort, and high integrity in keeping the country's most isolated communities connected with their loved ones.

Medium Answer Questions (11–20)

11. Explain how Khetaram performs his duty despite the harsh conditions of the Thar Desert.

Khetaram traverses several kilometers every day on foot across loose sand dunes carrying a mailbag that can weigh up to 28 kg. He defies blistering summer heat and harsh sandstorms without any shade. When temperatures become too hazardous, he smartly pauses and resumes walking after sunset, showing immense dedication.

12. How does the chapter highlight the importance of Gramin Dak Sewaks in rural India?

GDS workers act as the lifeline of rural communication. They bring mail, pensions, and financial money orders to remote hamlets, bridging the technological gap. Since many villagers are illiterate, they also act as translators and scribes, playing a major social role.

13. Why do the villagers have complete faith in Khetaram? Give reasons.

The villagers have absolute trust in Khetaram because he respects their privacy, reads and writes their letters with complete honesty, and delivers their hard-earned money safely. His helpful and sensitive nature has made him an indispensable family figure in the region.

14. How is Khetaram's job important for both his family and the villagers?

For his family, the GDS job provides a critical monthly cash income that saves them from agricultural losses caused by persistent droughts. For the villagers, Khetaram is the only reliable link to receive cash remittances and letters from relatives working in distant cities.

15. Describe the challenges faced by people living in the remote desert region.

The residents face an arid climate, frequent droughts, severe food and water shortages (famine), and a total lack of roads and modern transport networks. They live in scattered hamlets, making physical mail delivery a precious resource for communication.

16. Explain the importance of money orders in the lives of the villagers.

Because the desert has poor farming yields, many young men migrate to cities for work. They send money back home via money orders. These remittances are vital for the villagers to buy food, seeds, and pay for basic medical and family requirements.

17. What qualities of Khetaram make him an ideal Gramin Dak Sewak?

He is highly dedicated, honest, and physically resilient. He possesses immense empathy, treats the villagers' private letters with confidentiality, and is willing to change his schedule and walk at night to deliver his mail properly.

18. Why is delivering a letter carrying news of death the most difficult part of Khetaram's work?

Delivering news of death is emotionally draining. Khetaram has to break the tragic news, read the letter to the weeping family, and stay with them to offer comfort while maintaining local customs, which tests his emotional strength.

19. How has India Post helped connect remote villages?

India Post maintains a massive network of Gramin Dak Sewaks who physically reach areas where cellular signals or internet networks are weak or non-existent. GDS workers ensure that the benefit of governmental financial services and communications reaches the threshold of every rural household.

20. What message does the author convey through the chapter "Carrier of Words"?

The chapter conveys that true dignity lies in dedicated public service. It emphasizes that individuals like Khetaram, who quietly do their duty in harsh conditions without seeking fame, are the real heroes who hold society and human connections together.

Long Answer Questions (21–25)

21. Describe Khetaram's life and explain why he is called the "Carrier of Words".

Khetaram's life is defined by his long, difficult walks across the scorching Thar Desert. Carrying a mailbag weighing up to 28 kg on his shoulders, he journeys through remote hamlets near the international border. He is called the "Carrier of Words" because he does not merely deliver paper; he carries human feelings, hopes, and relationships. In a region cut off from modern digital communication, he delivers the words of migrant workers to their families. By reading these letters aloud and writing the replies on behalf of illiterate villagers, he ensures that thoughts and words continue to flow across hundreds of miles, serving as the voice of the community.

22. Explain the importance of Gramin Dak Sewaks in connecting remote communities with the rest of the country.

Gramin Dak Sewaks (GDS) play a critical, underappreciated role in rural and border communication in India. In places like the Thar Desert, where extreme weather, vast distances, and sand dunes prevent regular transportation, the GDS is often the only physical link to the outside world. They distribute pensions, government letters, and essential money orders, which keep the rural economy functioning. Beyond their official duties, GDS workers build a deep personal trust with families, acting as scribes and helpers. They ensure that even the most isolated citizens do not feel forgotten or cut off from the rest of the nation.

23. "People trusted Khetaram completely." Justify this statement with examples from the chapter.

The complete trust of the villagers in Khetaram is shown through several emotional interactions described in the chapter. Because many villagers are unable to read or write, they hand their personal mail directly to Khetaram, confident that he will keep their family secrets safe. They rely on him to handle their financial savings and deliver their money orders accurately. When Khetaram brings news, whether joyful or painful, they treat him as an honorary family member. Illiterate mothers and wives trust him to read letters from their sons and husbands and rely on him to write their exact feelings in reply, showcasing a deep human bond built on years of honest service.

24. What challenges does Khetaram face in his profession? How does he overcome them?

Khetaram faces massive physical and environmental challenges on a daily basis. He has to walk for miles over loose, shifting sand dunes under a scorching desert sun where temperatures rise above 45 degrees Celsius. He regularly deals with dry sandstorms, hot winds, and a lack of drinkable water. Additionally, he has to carry a heavy mailbag weighing up to 28 kg. To overcome these harsh conditions, Khetaram demonstrates great adaptability and resilience. During peak heatwaves, he pauses his journey during the day and walks under the cooler evening skies after sundown, ensuring the mail is delivered safely without compromising his health.

25. What message does the author convey through the chapter "Carrier of Words"? Explain with reference to Khetaram's character.

The author uses Khetaram’s character to deliver a powerful message about the beauty of selfless service, resilience, and compassion. Khetaram represents the silent heroes of our country who carry out their demanding duties in difficult situations without complaining or seeking praise. His character teaches us that a job is not just a source of income, but a way to help others and build human connections. His deep empathy, especially when dealing with letters of sorrow, and his perseverance in the desert show that dedication and kindness are essential values that keep communities united and strong.
